---
title: "Make the extension point cheaper than the fork"
summary: "The one architectural rule that kept a 40-site platform from becoming 40 codebases."
date: "2026-01"
tags: ["Architecture", "Design Systems"]
---

Every platform team eventually faces the same fork in the road — literally.
A consumer needs something the platform doesn't do, and they can either
extend it or fork it.

People don't fork because they're undisciplined. They fork because, at the
moment of decision, forking was cheaper:

1. The extension API was undocumented, or
2. it required a review cycle the fork didn't, or
3. it simply didn't exist yet.

So the rule: **whenever someone forks, treat it as a pricing bug in your
extension point.** Find the step that made extending more expensive than
copying, and delete that step.

On Corelane this meant a registry where a new block was one folder plus one
line, validated at build time. Once that landed, forks stopped — not because
we banned them, but because they became the slower option.
